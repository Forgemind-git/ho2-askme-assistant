Transferable Skills

User Interviewing

From: 8 years teaching secondary students

Synthesising Messy Feedback

From: Grading and assessment design

I spent years turning hundreds of test responses into clear patterns about what students did and didn't understand. The same affinity-mapping instinct turns raw research notes into themes a team can act on.

Communicating to Stakeholders

From: Parent meetings & staff briefings

Explaining a struggling student to anxious parents and a busy head of department taught me to tailor the same insight to very different audiences — exactly what research read-outs to PMs, designers and execs demand.

Designing for Real Behaviour

From: Lesson & curriculum planning

Every lesson is a usability test: plan it, watch where people get stuck, iterate next week. I learned to design around how people actually behave, not how I wished they would.

My Story

Where I started

I spent eight years as a secondary-school geography and humanities teacher, eventually leading a department of six. My job was understanding why a concept wasn't landing and redesigning how I taught it until it did.

The turning point

During the shift to remote learning I volunteered to help pilot a new digital learning platform. Running feedback sessions with students and writing up what confused them, I realised the part of teaching I loved most — figuring out how people think and removing their friction — was an entire profession: UX research.

Where I am heading

Over the past year I completed the Google UX Design Certificate and a dedicated UX research course, ran three volunteer research projects for a local non-profit's booking app, and built a portfolio of interview scripts, usability studies and research repositories. I'm now ready to do this full-time.

Ask Me Anything

Common questions about my career change — answered honestly.

You don't have a UX research job title yet — why should we hire you?
Because the core skills aren't new to me, only the label is. I've run thousands of "interviews", synthesised messy qualitative data into clear themes, and presented findings to demanding stakeholders for eight years. On top of that I've completed formal UX research training and shipped three real research projects with documented impact — you can read the write-ups in my portfolio.
Why the switch from teaching?
I'm not running from teaching — I'm running toward the part of it I loved most. The work that energised me was diagnosing why people got stuck and redesigning the experience until it clicked. UX research is that, full-time, for products that can reach far more people than one classroom ever could.
What have you actually done to prepare for this field?
Concretely: the Google UX Design Certificate, a mixed-methods UX research course, and three pro-bono studies for a local non-profit — including user interviews, a usability test on their booking flow, and an affinity-mapped findings report that changed their onboarding screen. I've also joined two UX research communities and do regular peer portfolio reviews.
