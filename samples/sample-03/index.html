About Me

How I use AI in my teaching

I teach Biology, Chemistry, and Physics to secondary school students across Year 7 to Year 13. AI has become an essential part of how I design lessons, support different learning styles, and keep content fresh.

📋

Lesson Plan Design

I give Claude the curriculum objective, the year group, and the available time, then ask it to draft a lesson plan with a hook, main activity, and exit ticket. I personalise it from there — saving 45 minutes of prep per lesson.

❓

Quiz & Assessment Questions

I ask Claude to generate GCSE-style multiple choice and short-answer questions on any topic, complete with mark schemes. This gives me a question bank I can mix and match for formative assessment.

💡

Plain-English Explainers

When students struggle with a concept like mitosis or electromagnetic induction, I ask Claude to re-explain it using an analogy suited to teenagers. I then read it aloud or display it on the board.

🎯

Differentiation & SEND Support

I use Claude to rewrite worksheets at different reading levels, generate alt-text descriptions for diagrams, and draft scaffolded sentence starters for students who need more structure.

Can you explain photosynthesis simply?
ASSISTANT
Here's a student-friendly explanation: Photosynthesis is how plants make their own food using sunlight. The recipe: • Ingredients: Carbon dioxide (from air) + Water (from soil) + Light energy (from the sun) • What's made: Glucose (food for the plant) + Oxygen (released into the air) Word equation: Carbon dioxide + Water → Glucose + Oxygen Memory hook: "Plants eat light and breathe out the stuff we breathe in." For Year 9+, introduce the chloroplast as the 'kitchen' where the reaction happens.
YOU
How do I differentiate for mixed-ability classes?
ASSISTANT
Three practical strategies: 1. Tiered tasks — Same learning objective, three versions: scaffold (sentence starters, word banks), core, and extension (application or evaluation question). Students self-select or you assign. 2. Question ladders — Start whole-class questions at recall level ("What is…?"), then push to application ("Why does…?"), then challenge ("Predict what would happen if…"). 3. Peer teaching — Pair high-prior-attainment students with developing learners for a structured 5-min explanation task. Explaining consolidates understanding for both.
YOU
Give me a starter activity for Year 9 chemistry
ASSISTANT
Try this 5-minute 'odd one out' starter: Show three substances: iron, sulfur, iron sulfide Prompt: "Which is the odd one out and why?" Possible student answers: • Iron — it's a pure metal (correct scientific angle) • Sulfur — it's a non-metal • Iron sulfide — it's a compound, not an element All three can be 'correct' depending on the property used — this sparks great discussion about elements, compounds, and mixtures, and reveals prior knowledge before the lesson starts.
